ANTHEMIAN TYPOLOGY

OCTI SYSTEM
Made by Blue Imagination

Welcome to Anthemian Typology, a framework for understanding how you're naturally wired to operate. Think of it as a user manual for your own mind. It gives you the language to understand your innate strengths and preferences. By exploring the five axes below, you can discover your unique approach to thinking, working, and living.

This test is from OMENVEIL Series by Blue Imagination. Here is how each lettering work if you're wondering:

I. How You Approach the World: Adaptive vs. Orderly

This describes your fundamental strategy for dealing with your surroundings. Are you someone who goes with the flow, or do you create the flow yourself?

Adaptive (A)

You are a master of flexibility. Your core strength is reacting to the world as it is, fluidly changing your approach to fit the situation. You see life as a dynamic river and excel by navigating its currents, not by trying to dam it.

  • You thrive on: Improvisation and spontaneity.
  • Your process is: Responsive and situational.
  • You excel in: Unpredictable, fast-changing environments where you have to think on your feet.

Orderly (O)

You are a natural architect of systems. Your core strength is structuring the world around you, creating plans and processes to ensure predictable, high-quality results. You see life as a system to be understood, organized, and optimized.

  • You thrive on: Procedures and planning.
  • Your process is: Proactive and systematic.
  • You excel in: Stable environments where precision, consistency, and long-term strategy are key.

II. How You Think: Convergent, Divergent, or Lateral

This axis reveals the "shape" of your thought process. When faced with a question, how does your mind naturally move to find an answer?

Convergent (C)

Your thinking is like a funnel. You excel at taking in a wide range of information, logically eliminating possibilities, and narrowing everything down to a single, correct answer. You are a natural problem-solver and analyst.

  • Your goal: Find the right answer.
  • Best for: Tasks requiring accuracy, data analysis, and logic (e.g., diagnosing a problem, solving a puzzle).

Divergent (D)

Your thinking is like a firework. You excel at starting from a single point and exploding outward into countless new ideas and possibilities. You are a natural brainstormer and innovator.

  • Your goal: Generate many new options.
  • Best for: Tasks requiring creativity and imagination (e.g., brainstorming new products, creating art).

Lateral (L)

Your thinking is like finding a hidden door. Instead of working within the existing framework, you sidestep the problem entirely, challenge the core assumptions, and come at it from a completely new angle. You are a true "out-of-the-box" thinker.

  • Your goal: Reframe the question itself.
  • Best for: Solving impossible problems and creating true paradigm shifts.

III. How You Get Things Done: Thinker vs. Worker

This defines where you feel the most satisfaction and sense of completion. Is the work done when the idea is perfect, or when the task is physically finished?

Thinking (T)

You are a conceptualizer. For you, the most important work happens in your mind. The "finished product" is a perfect plan, a brilliant strategy, or a complete theory. The physical execution is just a secondary step.

  • Satisfaction comes from: A flawless blueprint, a sound argument, or a moment of clear insight.
  • Motto: "Get the plan right, and the rest will follow."

Working (W)

You are an implementer. For you, ideas are just starting points. The real work is in the application—the process of building, creating, and making a tangible impact on the world.

  • Satisfaction comes from: A finished product, a completed task, or a visible result.
  • Motto: "An idea is worthless until you make it real."

IV. What Is Your Method of Knowing Information: Public vs. Quiet

This describes how you get your day to day information. Do you gain them from being around others, or do you need solitude to search it for yourself?

Public (P)

You are energized by external interaction. You often think best out loud, bouncing ideas off others and drawing stimulation from social settings. Being with people charges your brain up. A Public person gathers information by exposure.

  • You process information: Externally, through discussion, with others.
  • You feel best: In collaborative and active environments.

Quiet (Q)

You are energized by internal reflection. You do your best thinking in your own head, processing things deeply before you speak. Solitude is what charges your brain up. A Quiet (Q) person is someone who gathers information by internalisation.

  • You process information: Internally, through introspection, by yourself.
  • You feel best: In calm environments where you have space to think.

Note

In the Anthemian system, Public (P) and Quiet (Q) are not the same as being extroverted or introverted. They describe where your energy flows and how you orient yourself in a group. It has nothing to do with shyness. Both can be scientists, artists, or leaders — they just operate with opposite data-gathering instincts.

A Public (P) type feels comfortable stepping forward, leading, coordinating, or setting the social rhythm. They tend to think through interaction and draw stability from the presence of others. Being “Public” doesn’t sincerely mean you’re loud or attention-seeking, it's just that your energy aligns with participation and guidance. Public type may even prefer to be alone and be independent depending on the person, but what making them Public is that they are confident enough to step and debate their point comfortably for hours, lead without many thinking, and more than happy when someone wants to follow them.

A very introverted Public (iP) might not want to socialise much, but when they must — say, in a debate or a presentation — they come alive intellectually. They gain focus from engagement and genuinely looks happy doing so, even if they later rather sleep than going to parties. The act of presenting structured thought energizes them but the unstructured chaos of a party will drain them.

A Quiet (Q) type, meanwhile, usually prefers to move within a small trusted circle. They process things internally, and leadership feels unnatural or even draining. A Quiet person can be charismatic, outspoken, and confident, yet still prefer to observe, reflect, and contribute rather than direct, fearing what they say might make a situation worse. They are more likely to feel awkward when suddenly put into a position where they have to lead a team, or even one person. They are skilled at navigating social currents but are not usually equipped to direct them, and the responsibility is a significant source of stress.

An extroverted Quiet (eQ) enjoys people and can perform socially, but they burn energy doing so and need alone-time to refuel. They’re that friend who’s radiant at a party, then disappears for three days afterwards to recover.

V. Your Inner Climate: Cyclothymic vs. Schizothymic

This axis describes the underlying rhythm of your emotional and energetic world. Is your inner landscape dynamic and fluctuating, or is it stable and consistent?

Cyclothymic (SL)

Your temperament is warm and dynamic. You have a naturally fluctuating emotional energy, with cycles of high enthusiasm and periods of quiet sensitivity. This makes you highly attuned to the emotional and personal side of life.

  • Disposition: People-oriented, expressive, and vibrant.
  • Inner World: Like a tide, with a natural rhythm of highs and lows.

Schizothymic (SK)

Your temperament is cool and stable. You have a consistent emotional baseline, allowing for immense focus and objectivity. You tend to engage with the world through logic and ideas rather than feelings.

  • Disposition: Object-oriented, analytical, and reserved.
  • Inner World: Like a calm, deep lake, consistent and ideational.
